I have decided to write about a topic I was forced to become familiar with over the past 3 years: US taxes and US government incentivized ways to minimize them.

This blog will probably be most helpful to someone who is unfamiliar with these tax deductions, saving in the tens of thousands a year, and wants to avoid taxes. The savings per year compound and can have an effect in the low hundreds of thousands by retirement assuming you start in early 20s. DISCLAIMER: This is not financial advice, and you should do your own research before opening any of these accounts. One thing to note is that as long as the money is in the tax-advantaged account, any gains will be tax free. It is only when you start withdrawing that there may be tax implications.

HSA: can only open this if you have an High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP, as hinted at by the name, a plan with a high deductible as opposed to a PPO plan) it is pretax and This is technically the most tax advantaged account that I know of

401k 529, saves on state tax Roth IRA: backdoor roth ira

* 401k - Empower Retirement contributions are tax deductible money grows tax free traditional 401(k) withdrawals are taxed at an individual's income tax rate at the time of withdrawal no access until minimum age 55 If you quit, you pile this money into a traditional IRA.

* HSA - BenefitWallet contributions are tax-deductible money grows tax-free money can come out tax free (as long as its used for health purposes... once you turn 65, you can use it for whatever)

* IRA - Vanguard must do post-tax traditional IRA (no tax benefits) to roth IRA backdoor (tax exempt) contributions are *not* tax deductible money grows tax free money can come out tax free no access until 59.5

* 529 - BrightStart contributions are Illinois state income tax-deductible up to $10k money can come out tax free One can contribute 15k before gift tax One can switch beneficiaries tax free, although there are federal gift tax consequences DRIP - Dividend Reinvestment Program
